PAPER 10: MegaScale-Infer: Efficient Mixture-of-Experts Model Serving
with Disaggregated Expert Parallelism
1

Paper summary

Serving large-scale Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models faces severe inefficiency during decoding.
Because MoE dynamically routes tokens to sparse experts, Feed-Forward Networks (FFNs)
suffer from small effective batch sizes, shifting from compute-intensive to memory-intensive.
This leads to “substantially lower GPU utilization and increased operational costs”.
To solve this, MegaScale-Infer utilizes “disaggregated expert parallelism”. Separating attention and FFN modules allows independent scaling of attention nodes to aggregate larger
batches for experts, enabling “heterogeneous deployment” to match each module with costeffective hardware.
To hide the new communication latency, the system employs “ping-pong pipeline parallelism” to shuttle micro-batches between modules, alongside a custom “M2N communication
library”. Evaluated on Mixtral 8x22B and DBRX, it achieved up to a “1.90x higher per-GPU
throughput” on homogeneous setups and a 1.86x per-cost throughput improvement on heterogeneous clusters.
2

Contributions

A primary contribution of this work is the introduction of “disaggregated expert parallelism”
for MoE serving. By separating attention and FFN modules, it makes it possible to independently scale the memory-bound attention layers and compute-bound expert layers. This
dramatically improves overall GPU utilization and enables highly cost-effective heterogeneous
hardware deployments.
To facilitate this decoupled architecture, the authors design “ping-pong pipeline parallelism”.
By dividing global request batches into smaller micro-batches and continuously shuttling them
between the attention and expert nodes, this technique successfully overlaps network communication with GPU computation, effectively hiding the latency overhead introduced by disaggregation.
Furthermore, the research presents a high-performance “M2N communication library” tailored specifically for MoE token routing. By avoiding “unnecessary GPU-to-CPU data copies,
group initialization overhead, and GPU synchronization”, it significantly improves communication reliability, achieving 4.2x higher throughput and 68.2% lower median latency compared to
standard libraries like NCCL.
3

Problems

One significant limitation is the approach’s sensitivity to workload imbalances and pipeline bubbles. The ping-pong pipeline requires a precise balance of computation times between attention
and expert nodes. Since real-world attention computation times vary wildly across sequence
lengths, imbalances can easily “introduce bubbles and degrade overall system efficiency” if not
perfectly managed.
Another concern lies in the strict network deployment requirements. Disaggregating layers
turns fast intra-node memory accesses into expensive inter-node M2N transfers. Consequently,
the system heavily relies on an optimized CPU-proxy communication architecture using RDMA,
meaning it may scale poorly on standard cloud instances lacking high-end InfiniBand setups.
Finally, the architecture struggles with extreme expert load volatility during the prefill
phase. While load distribution is relatively stable during decoding, the authors note that
expert popularity “fluctuates more significantly” during prefill. This forces the system to rely
on frequent plan adjustments and risks overwhelming hot experts, which could cause massive
tail-latency spikes under sudden traffic shifts.
