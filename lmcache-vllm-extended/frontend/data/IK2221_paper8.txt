PAPER 8: Taming Throughput-Latency Tradeoff in LLM Inference with
Sarathi-Serve
1

Paper summary

LLM inference serving faces a ”throughput-latency tradeoff” between its compute-bound prefill
phase and memory-bound decode phase. Interleaving these phases inherently causes ”generation
stalls”, where lengthy prefills delay ongoing decodes and produce unacceptable tail latencies.
Furthermore, in multi-node setups, variance in execution times creates ”pipeline bubbles” that
”waste GPU cycles”.
To tackle this challenge, the authors propose Sarathi-Serve, an inference scheduler centered around ”chunked-prefills” and ”stall-free scheduling”. Instead of processing massive input
prompts entirely at once, the system divides the prefill sequence into smaller, near-equal chunks.
By piggybacking these chunks onto ongoing decode batches, Sarathi-Serve ensures each iteration
has similar workload, which avoids idle GPU time and improves efficiency
Evaluations under tail latency constraints show Sarathi-Serve significantly outperforms baselines. It delivers up to ”2.6x higher serving capacity” for Mistral-7B on a single A100 GPU
and up to a ”5.6x gain in the end-to-end serving capacity” in pipeline-parallel deployments by
effectively eliminating generation stalls and mitigating pipeline bubbles.
2

Contributions

The paper makes three primary contributions. First, it introduces LLM scheduling via ”chunkedprefills” and ”stall-free batching”. This approach seamlessly merges compute-bound prefill
chunks with memory-bound decodes, achieving high batching throughput while strictly minimizing the impact on ”time-between-tokens” (TBT) latency.
Second, it exposes pitfalls in ”prefill-prioritizing” policies and mitigates ”pipeline bubbles”
in iteration-level scheduling. By ensuring uniform micro-batch computation, the method saves
wasted GPU cycles, making it feasible to efficiently deploy and scale massive models using
”pipeline-parallelism” across commodity networks.
Finally, extensive evaluations across diverse hardware and leading models (e.g., Mistral-7B,
Falcon-180B) prove the system’s generality. By operating effectively under strict ”Service Level
Objective (SLO)” constraints, the research offers improved reliability for real-world applications
and increases end-to-end serving capacity by ”up to an order of magnitude”.
3

Problems

Despite its significant contributions, the paper exhibits a few notable limitations regarding
deployment assumptions and evaluation scope. First, Sarathi-Serve relies heavily on a statically
determined ”token budget” derived from offline profiling and simulation. In highly dynamic
production environments where request lengths and arrival rates fluctuate unpredictably, a
fixed budget configuration may become sub-optimal, a constraint the authors acknowledge by
leaving dynamic budget adjustments to future work.
Second, the chunking mechanism involves an inherent trade-off regarding initial model responsiveness. Because large input prompts are fragmented and computed across multiple iterations, the ”time-to-first-token” (TTFT) latency is slightly degraded compared to prefillprioritizing systems or disaggregated architectures that execute full prefills with maximum efficiency in a single pass.
Finally, the evaluation lacks a direct quantitative comparison with emerging disaggregated
LLM serving architectures, such as ”Splitwise” or ”DistServe”. Because disaggregated systems
physically isolate the prefill and decode phases onto different replicas to ”entirely eliminate
the interference” , the absence of this baseline leaves it somewhat ambiguous how SarathiServe’s iteration-level batching truly performs and scales against these fundamentally different
deployment paradigms.
