PAPER 4: Metron: NFV Service Chains at the True Speed of the Underlying
Hardware
1

Paper summary

Achieving high throughput and low latency for Network Functions Virtualization (NFV) service
chains on commodity hardware is traditionally difficult because dispatching packets in multicore servers ”can cause costly inter-core transfers” and load imbalances.
Metron solves this by ”jointly exploiting the underlying network and commodity servers
resources” to eliminate inter-core communication entirely. It does this by synthesizing service
chains into an optimized processing graph and offloading stateless operations directly to network switches. These switches apply smart tags to specific traffic classes, allowing the server’s
Network Interface Card (NIC) to dispatch incoming packets directly to the exact CPU core
responsible for the remaining stateful software processing. To handle workload fluctuations,
Metron’s controller can dynamically rebalance the CPU load by splitting or merging these
tagged traffic classes across different cores.
In evaluations using real-world network traces on commodity servers and OpenFlow switches,
”Metron has 2.75-6.5x better efficiency than OpenBox”, a state-of-the-art NFV system. By bypassing software dispatching overheads, Metron successfully ”realizes stateful network functions
at the speed of a 100 GbE network card on a single server” , delivering ”up to 4.7x lower latency,
up to 7.8x higher throughput”.
2

Contributions

First, the paper presents a system that jointly exploits programmable switches, NICs, and
CPUs to ”offload part of the packet processing logic to the network”. This significantly reduces
software overhead and enables efficient use of heterogeneous hardware resources.
Second, it introduces a novel tagging and hardware-based dispatching mechanism that ensures packets are directed to the correct CPU core from the moment they enter the server.
By enforcing traffic affinity at the hardware level, Metron ”eliminates inter-core transfers” and
enables processing ”at the speed of the servers’ fastest cache(s)”.
Third, the paper proposes a novel resource allocation scheme ”that minimizes the resource
allocation overhead for large-scale NFV deployments”. Using lightweight heuristics such as
”the power of two random choices”, Metron achieves scalable and adaptive load balancing while
minimizing monitoring and coordination overhead.
3

Problems

Despite its strong performance, Metron has several limitations. First, its state management
strategy relies on a scheme that ”quickly duplicates the stateful tables of a group of traffic
classes across the involved CPU cores” during load redistribution. This can lead to memory
inefficiency and temporary inconsistencies, especially under frequent scaling operations.
Second, the system depends on intrusive tagging mechanisms that repurpose Layer 2 fields
such as MAC addresses or VLAN IDs. This imposes restrictive deployment requirements and
may conflict with existing network configurations, limiting real-world applicability.
Third, Metron’s placement strategy trades accuracy for scalability by using randomized
heuristics. While efficient, it only matches optimal placement decisions with limited probability,
which may lead to suboptimal resource utilization in large or highly dynamic environments.
Finally, the design assumes the availability of programmable switches and advanced NIC
features. This reliance on specific hardware capabilities may hinder adoption in environments
where such support is limited or unavailable.
